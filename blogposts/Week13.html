Digital Divide Widens


The article highlights the significant impact of the Covid-19 pandemic on education and the widening digital divide. It emphasizes that over “1.5 billion students” (Glynos & Tataki, 2020). globally have been affected by school closures, leading to a shift towards e-learning. However, a considerable number of students lack access to computers and do not have any form of internet access at home. The digital divide is challenging educators in ensuring continuous learning even with the existing inequalities. This is particularly evident in low-income countries, where a large majority of students are unable to go online. It gives short-term solutions like lending laptops or providing free SIM cards and data usage to learn but they are not sufficient to bridge the gap. To prevent increasing inequalities and the digital divide from going further users or students must be provided with high-quality and affordable internet access to all.


Gender Shades: Intersectional Accuracy Disparities in Commercial Gender Classification


The article highlights the issue of bias in machine learning algorithms, specifically in the context of facial analysis algorithms. As in the article, they evaluated to assess bias in automated facial analysis algorithms and found significant inequalities in the distribution of gender and skin types. In the article, it emphasizes the need for attention and action to address the inequalities in the accuracy of gender classification systems. They argue that if commercial companies want to develop facial analysis algorithms that are genuinely fair, transparent, and accountable, they must address these inequalities and biases and present them in their algorithms. As of now, their algorithms are unable to accurately identify darker-skinned people, more specifically darker-skinned women. An example of why this is a problem is that some companies make use of AI in job application screens. If an AI is unable to “classify” a person or they do not fit the standards in terms of race or gender, they are rejected.

Overall, the importance of understanding and addressing bias in machine learning algorithms, especially in facial analysis systems, is to ensure e unbiased outcomes for all individuals, regardless of their race or gender.
